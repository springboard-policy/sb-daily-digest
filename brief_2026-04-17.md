## Skills Policy & Workforce Development

1. **[Ontario Budget 2026: A Capability Assessment](https://cdhowe.org/publication/ontario-budget-2026-a-capability-assessment/)** — C.D. Howe senior policy analyst Harvey Naglie finds Ontario's $13.8B deficit budget identifies the right strategic sectors (nuclear, critical minerals, life sciences, defence manufacturing, shipbuilding) but warns its core instruments — non-repayable grants without performance conditions, tax deferrals tied to calendar dates rather than capability milestones, and Buy Ontario procurement without supplier development obligations — risk compensating for industrial weakness rather than building durable capacity.
   *The critique lands precisely at the intersection of industrial policy and workforce development: procurement protectionism without skills conditionality produces client firms, not export-competitive ones — and the ECE and trades workforce gaps that underpin Ontario's labour-supply constraints go unaddressed in the budget's capability framing.*

Howe AI competition policy work covered Thursday (April 15) — that brief called for government to be a pro-competitive force through open-source and compute access; Enoch's case study shows the inverse, where public infrastructure decisions are being made with less accountability than private sector transactions, setting a troubling precedent for AI governance across the country.*

## Northern & Arctic Infrastructure

1. **[Gjoa Haven, Nunavut, lifts state of emergency after hamlet reveals it had no emergency plan during 3-day power outage](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/gjoa-haven-nunavut-lifts-state-of-emergency-1.7513890)** — The hamlet of Gjoa Haven declared and then lifted a state of emergency following a three-day power outage, with CBC North subsequently reporting the community had no emergency plan in place when the crisis struck; the hamlet said "conditions have improved" without providing detail on what systemic gaps had been addressed.
   *Power outages in Arctic communities are a persistent and predictable emergency risk, and the absence of a formal emergency plan in an isolated Nunavut hamlet underscores the infrastructure governance gap that federal Arctic defence and sovereignty investments — including the $35–40B package announced in February — have yet to translate into community-level resilience; this is exactly the kind of dual-use infrastructure need (emergency management + sovereignty) that APPA and northern Indigenous organizations have flagged.*

2. **[Liard First Nation slams 'total failure' on Yukon mine consultation](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/liard-first-nation-yukon-mine-consultation-1.7512800)** — Liard First Nation has publicly condemned Yukon's consultation process for a proposed mine development, calling it a "total failure" and signalling potential legal challenge; the specific mine and procedural failures were described in the CBC North excerpt.
   *The Liard First Nation's intervention echoes a pattern of consultation failures at the Yukon–First Nations interface that has recurred across multiple resource projects — and arrives as the Carney majority government is expected to advance Arctic sovereignty and resource frameworks that will require demonstrably improved free, prior, and informed consent processes to withstand constitutional scrutiny.*

3. **[Yukon government offers incentives to switch to fossil fuels amid electric bill rebates](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/yukon-government-incentives-fossil-fuels-electric-bills-rebates-1.7513200)** — The Yukon government is offering financial incentives to residents to switch away from electricity toward fossil fuels, alongside rebates on electric bills, in what appears to be a dual policy response to affordability pressures driven by high electricity costs in the territory.
   *The move runs counter to the federal clean energy transition agenda and illustrates the structural energy affordability challenge unique to remote northern grids — where the economics of electrification remain inverted relative to southern Canada — putting Yukon in tension with federal climate commitments in ways that will need to be resolved in upcoming territorial transfer negotiations.*

4. **[Ross River residents urge Yukon government to replace condemned pool](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/ross-river-yukon-condemned-pool-1.7513100)** — Residents of Ross River, Yukon, are pressing the territorial government to replace a condemned community pool, describing the loss as "heartbreaking" for a remote community where recreational infrastructure is central to youth wellbeing and social cohesion.
   *Remote community recreational infrastructure in Yukon sits at the intersection of northern social infrastructure and capital renewal gaps — the Yukon government's capacity to respond is constrained by the same fiscal pressures driving its fossil fuel incentive pivot, and federal infrastructure programming has historically not adequately addressed small community facility replacement in the territories.*
