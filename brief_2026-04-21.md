## Skills Policy & Workforce Development

1. **[Youth facing 'perfect storm' in job hunt this summer, economists say](https://fsc-ccf.ca/engage/youth-facing-perfect-storm-in-job-hunt-this-summer-economists-say/)** — FSC Director of Research Tricia Williams and economists at Indeed warn that Canada's "low-fire, low-hire" economy, driven by tariff-induced uncertainty and cooling consumer demand, is creating the worst summer youth job market in over a decade, with the 15–24 unemployment rate stuck near 14% and the youth employment rate hitting its lowest point since 1998 last September.
   *The piece explicitly links public youth employment programs to equity outcomes — noting young people without parental networks face compounding disadvantage — making this a timely evidence base for ESDC and provincial governments to justify renewed Canada Summer Jobs and targeted youth labour market programming in the upcoming federal budget.*

2. **[Inuvik set to lose 13 teachers next year after layoffs, union president says](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/inuvik-teacher-layoffs-2026-1.7518800)** — The NWT Teachers' Association president has flagged that 13 teaching positions in Inuvik will be cut for the 2026–27 school year, a significant blow to an already understaffed northern education system.
   *This directly echoes the April 15 CCPA analysis of Ontario school staffing erosion — but in a northern context where teacher recruitment and retention is structurally harder and the consequences for Indigenous and remote students are more severe; it signals a national pattern of education workforce attrition that federal and territorial governments have not yet addressed at scale.*

## Northern & Arctic Infrastructure

1. **[Flood risk 'above average' for much of Yukon, with lots of snow yet to melt](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/yukon-flood-risk-above-average-2026-1.7518900)** — The Yukon Government has issued a flood and wildfire seasonal outlook warning of above-average flood risk across much of the territory, with snowpack levels that remain elevated heading into the melt season.
   *This follows the April 20 brief's item on the Yukon releasing final flood hazard maps for Dawson and the Klondike Valley — that planning tool and this seasonal alert together signal an active infrastructure resilience challenge for Yukon communities, and the combination of high flood risk and wildfire season amplifies pressure on territorial emergency management capacity that has historically been underfunded.*

2. **[No more daylight saving: Northwest Territories to end seasonal time change](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/north/northwest-territories-end-daylight-saving-time-1.7519000)** — The NWT Legislative Assembly has voted to eliminate daylight saving time, making the territory the latest Canadian jurisdiction to adopt permanent standard time.
   *While procedurally modest, the move is notable as a signal of territorial policy autonomy on quality-of-life and health issues and aligns with growing cross-jurisdictional consensus in Canada and the US around ending the time change — Saskatchewan has operated on permanent standard time for decades, and B.C., Ontario and other provinces have passed enabling legislation pending federal coordination.*

## Social Assistance & Income Security

1. **[When mental health conditions and physical disabilities co-occur: Examining service needs](https://www.wellesleyinstitute.com/when-mental-health-conditions-and-physical-disabilities-co-occur-examining-service-needs/)** — A new Wellesley Institute / CMHA Toronto report drawing on Ontario's Common Assessment of Need (OCAN) dataset finds that community mental health clients with co-occurring physical disabilities have significantly higher unmet needs across physical health, self-care, transportation, psychological distress, and social connection compared to those with mental health conditions alone.
   *The findings make a concrete, data-grounded case for integrated disability and mental health service planning — directly relevant to the Canada Disability Benefit's implementation design, where the Carney government has yet to finalize the regulatory framework for benefit delivery and service coordination, and underscores that income support alone will be insufficient without complementary service access.*
