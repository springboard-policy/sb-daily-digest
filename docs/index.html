Northern & Arctic Infrastructure

  1. Yukon government offers incentives to switch to fossil fuels and rebates on electric bills — The Yukon government is offering financial incentives for residents to switch back to fossil fuel heating and providing rebates on electricity bills, a policy reversal that reflects the territory's acute energy affordability pressures as diesel generation costs spike.

    Analysis: This runs directly counter to the federal clean energy transition agenda and highlights a recurring tension in northern energy policy: electrification targets designed for southern grids impose disproportionate costs on remote diesel-dependent communities, a dynamic that the Carney government's Arctic infrastructure envelope will need to explicitly address.

  2. Liard First Nation slams 'total failure' on Yukon mine consultation — Liard First Nation has publicly condemned the Yukon government's consultation process on a mine project as a "total failure," signalling a serious breakdown in the duty-to-consult relationship between the territorial government and an affected First Nation.

    Analysis: Duty-to-consult failures at the territorial level are increasingly generating litigation and project delays across the North; this follows a pattern of Yukon resource development disputes and will be watched closely as Ottawa advances its critical minerals strategy, for which Yukon is a priority jurisdiction.

  3. Gjoa Haven, Nunavut, lifts state of emergency as conditions improve — The hamlet of Gjoa Haven lifted its state of emergency after a three-day power outage, with separate CBC reporting revealing the hamlet had no emergency plan in place during the crisis.

    Analysis: The absence of a community emergency plan in a Nunavut hamlet of roughly 1,400 people underscores the chronic infrastructure governance gap in remote Arctic communities — the same vulnerability that the $35–40B Arctic defence investment package (announced February) is oriented around strategically, but which requires community-level emergency preparedness investment to be operationally meaningful.

  4. Alberta oil and gas company leaves $476M cleanup bill — The Alberta Energy Regulator has confirmed 4,031 wells, 383 facilities, and 2,121 pipeline segments from Long Run Exploration Ltd. are now orphans, with a sworn court affidavit estimating cleanup liability at $476.8M — the largest single transfer to the Orphan Well Association on record, involving a Chinese holding company whose investors were unfamiliar with Canadian environmental liability law.

    Analysis: The scale of this liability — nearly quadrupling the Orphan Well Association's annual decommissioning budget of $130M in a single announcement — reinforces the University of Calgary law professor's assessment that the industry-funded insurance model is "wholly inadequate," and sets up a significant federal-provincial debate about taxpayer exposure as critical minerals and energy security investment attracts new foreign capital to Alberta.
