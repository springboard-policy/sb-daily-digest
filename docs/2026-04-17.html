Northern & Arctic Infrastructure

  1. LNG Canada facility burned more gas in 2025 than the estimated global record for any LNG terminal in 2024 — The Narwhal/Point Source investigation finds LNG Canada (Kitimat, B.C.) flared 350 million cubic metres of gas in 2025 — roughly 50% above the prior estimated global record (Nigeria's Bonny Island, 234M m³ in 2024) — with 10% of all gas transported to the facility burned off, flaring consistently exceeding provincial permit limits, and mechanical problems expected to persist three to five years.

    Analysis: This directly undermines Canada's "cleanest LNG in the world" positioning used to market exports to Asian and European markets, and creates regulatory and reputational exposure for a project central to the federal government's energy-corridor and trade-diversification agenda — with particular relevance to B.C. First Nations communities in the Kitimat region.

  2. $476M orphan well liability lands on Alberta industry fund after Chinese-backed Long Run Exploration collapses — Alberta's Energy Regulator has formally orphaned 4,031 wells, 383 facilities, and 2,121 pipeline segments from Long Run Exploration Ltd., adding an estimated $476M cleanup liability to the Orphan Well Association — which spent only $130M on decommissioning all of Alberta in 2023 — after a $22M sale to a Chinese buyer fell through.

    Analysis: University of Calgary law professor Shaun Fluker calls this "the result of regulatory failure," noting the OWA levy system was never designed for liabilities of this scale; with the OWA already carrying 4,200 orphan wells before this transfer, the growing probability of public bailout has direct implications for federal–provincial resource policy and the critical-minerals agenda discussed in the Ontario budget above.
