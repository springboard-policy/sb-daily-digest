Skills Policy & Workforce Development

  1. FSC Research: Most Canadian Workers Support EDI — "Backlash" Narrative Overstated — A new Future Skills Centre report, EDI Backlash? What Canadian Workers Really Think, drawing on a national survey of 5,603 workers, finds 54% of Canadian employees view workplace EDI positively, only 16% negatively, and nearly half report their employer's EDI approach has benefited their own opportunities. (FSC) The finding that EDI opposition is concentrated among economically insecure workers — not a broad majority — is significant counter-evidence to the political framing that has prompted some Canadian employers to quietly scale back inclusion programs in response to U.S. corporate rollbacks; it strengthens the evidence base for organizations defending EDI programming in government and employer settings.

  2. C.D. Howe: Express Entry Worked — But Temporary Resident Earnings Deterioration Threatens Gains — A new C.D. Howe Commentary (Selection Matters, Skuterud & Zhang) finds that post-2015 Express Entry reforms improved labour market outcomes for permanent residents — with recent male cohorts approaching earnings parity with Canadian-born workers within five years — but documents a sharp deterioration in earnings among non-permanent resident men (2020–2024), particularly college-educated workers. (C.D. Howe) With the Carney government's immigration reduction targets expected to turn population growth negative in coming years, this report arrives as a direct brief to Ottawa: return to exclusive reliance on the Comprehensive Ranking System and resist using temporary-resident-to-PR pathways as a volume mechanism, or risk dismantling a decade of hard-won gains in immigrant labour market integration.

  3. C.D. Howe: $10/Day Childcare Not Delivering Promised Spaces — Structural Fix Needed — A new C.D. Howe podcast and associated report (From Promise to Practice) by Mahboubi and Zhang finds that while fee reductions have improved affordability, Canada's promised 250,000+ new childcare spaces have not materialized, with demand substantially outpacing supply and families stuck on waitlists. (C.D. Howe) This adds to growing evidence — including from FINA pre-budget submissions — that the federal childcare program's design prioritizes demand-side subsidies over supply-side capacity building; the Carney majority now faces pressure to address the structural gap before the next federal budget.

  4. CCPA: Ontario's Northlander Rideshare Pilot a Gig-Work Wage Subsidy for Uber — CCPA analysis reveals that Ontario's upcoming Northlander Rideshare Pilot — framed as rural transit support alongside the Northlander train revival — will extend the Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act's "engaged time only" wage model provincewide, with U of T research showing Uber drivers' median after-expense earnings at $5.97/hour in 2024. The pilot, led by Cabinet Office staff and linked to Uber's lobbyist Rubicon, is expected to launch summer 2026 as a template for a 2027 province-wide framework. (CCPA) This is the most concrete provincial development in the ongoing platform/gig work policy debate; the provincewide standardization timeline means federal and provincial advocates for gig worker protections have a narrow window ahead of the 2027 framework rollout — and it sets a low-road precedent that other provinces may follow.

LNG Canada Flared More Gas in 2025 Than Any Recorded LNG Terminal Globally in 2024 — An investigation by The Narwhal and Point Source finds LNG Canada burned 350 million cubic metres of gas in 2025 — roughly 50% above estimates for the world's highest-flaring LNG terminal in 2024 (Nigeria's Bonny Island) — with repeated exceedances of provincial permits and ongoing mechanical failures at the $40B Kitimat facility.   2. Carney Government's Access-to-Information Reforms Draw Sharp Pushback — The Narwhal reports that Treasury Board's March 5 proposal to redefine "official records" — potentially shielding routine emails and text messages from ATI requests — has prompted the Information Commissioner and public access advocates to call on a parliamentary committee to "urgently" review the changes before they advance; the public feedback period runs to June 15. (The Narwhal) Restricting what constitutes an official government record would most directly harm researchers, journalists, and advocacy organizations that rely on ATI to document policy implementation gaps — including in income support, Indigenous services, and disability programming — making this a sleeper issue for accountability-focused civil society organizations.
