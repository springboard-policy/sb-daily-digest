## Skills Policy & Workforce Development

1. **[Canada's need for skilled trades workers will far outstrip boost from Ottawa's new plan](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMihwJBVV95cUxNTnVvS1N3VFFuWm5XQWgzSHl6R0tmTDQtUkgwT1ZuSDlOZ0R2N1BQR1kyWmhzVWtNdTJQRl81ZU9aRU9lRmwydGh1WHN5ckprTk5BRXl1U0pzYzc5NU9oR2NnRTN4R0o1ZHJWM3U3dkVNQjVnbktHTFJfZTRFLWFSajhvWVNBRHpnR1Boc0Vwb2N4eGlsQzdrc1VFRlI4NXhRRE1ldEprRnFpX01Kb3h3MUFpMGkwbVQ5YUlUVlFRUHE1Y3lUYVNOOE5lMUNtQ0pXeU5xNWJ0eVpHZUZERThZLXlubExNSjh0VWZhb3NJR0pvbzJsX2xKMXJYRjFFenptbjV2TjZIUQ?oc=5)** — The Toronto Star reports that industry analysts and labour economists are warning that the federal government's $6 billion "Team Canada Strong" trades plan, announced in Tuesday's Spring Economic Update, falls well short of the scale needed to close Canada's projected skilled trades shortfall over the next decade.
   *The critique sharpens the policy debate immediately after the SEU announcement: if the 80,000–100,000 Red Seal target by 2031 is materially below industry demand projections, it raises design questions about whether the program's $16,000 per-apprentice grants and employer incentives are structured to maximize uptake or merely to demonstrate political commitment — a distinction that FINA submissions due today will be probing.*

2. **[Housing minister says Spring Economic Update "meets the moment" on homebuilding](https://ipolitics.ca/2026/04/29/gregor-robertson-spring-economic-update-ottawa-housing-canada/)**  — Housing and Infrastructure Minister Gregor Robertson defended the SEU's trades investment on Wednesday, framing the $6 billion "Team Canada Strong" package as a necessary workforce foundation for housing, infrastructure, and defence delivery, and confirming the government is separately examining temporary foreign worker pathways to supplement domestic trades training.
   *Robertson's reference to TFW policy as a parallel trades lever signals that the Carney government views immigration and domestic training as complementary rather than competing tools — a framing that will be tested against the April 16 C.D. Howe finding that NPR earnings deterioration is already eroding the gains from Express Entry's selectivity improvements.*

3. **[ILO Unveils Global Report on Skills and Lifelong Learning](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMihwFBVV95cUxNLUQ0MHMwV0Y4VzV6RHlCbkxWb2NxaEZjXy1RblB3MEw1U3RTNUxuNDU5Yk50TnNPZEgtMGNUdjhOLVptRGZLakpfRTZEdlNRUThpYkNGNFNDLXp3c0g2czQ0aFFreVJDNGtDZmN0SFd0YzM3TVJWX20wNG4teTZEODZnY0l5TWs?oc=5)** — The ILO has released a major new global report on skills and lifelong learning, examining how countries can build adaptive training systems capable of responding to technological disruption, demographic change, and the green transition — calling for integrated national skills strategies that link training finance, employer engagement, and social protection.
   *The report's framework — linking skills finance to social protection systems — is directly relevant to Canada's post-SEU policy debate: the Carney government's $6B trades commitment is largely supply-side (training grants, completions bonuses) but does not yet address the income security floor for workers in training, a gap that EI reform advocates and FINA submitters have consistently flagged as the missing link in Canadian workforce development policy.*

4. **[FSC Director of Research Tricia Williams on the Spring Economic Update and skilled trades](https://fsc-ccf.ca/engage/tricia-williams-discusses-spring-economic-update-the-house-with-catherine-cullen/)** — FSC Director of Research Tricia Williams appeared on CBC Radio's *The House* and *The Current* to provide independent expert commentary on the federal government's Spring Economic Update trades strategy, underscoring workforce equity dimensions and the need for supports targeting young people without existing networks into the trades.
   *Williams' consistent emphasis on equity access — particularly for youth without parental connections to the trades — positions FSC as a credible independent voice in the post-SEU implementation debate, and aligns with the April 21 FSC youth employment brief's finding that the "low-fire, low-hire" economy compounds disadvantage for the most marginalized young workers.*

## Northern & Arctic Infrastructure

1. **[Tłı̨chǫ Government and Fortune Mining to form joint venture for 50 km access road](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMiUEFVX3lxTE81dDJnU2tSVHU0UkwtdWhocDZTcVZ2eEJiXy1HZjdzMkFJbFJhTzdmVmJJNWphWUtvN25TQXplQVFVdWttb0dVTjdZWUdFZFBV?oc=5)** — The Tłı̨chǫ Government and Fortune Mining have announced a joint venture to build a 50-kilometre access road in the Northwest Territories, an Indigenous-led infrastructure partnership model that would provide the territory with a new mineral corridor.
   *This is a concrete example of the Indigenous equity participation model in northern resource infrastructure that the April 20 brief flagged with NWT's hydro ownership story — a First Nation entering as a joint venture partner in road construction (rather than merely a consultation party) represents a more substantive form of self-determination, and sets a precedent relevant to the Carney government's stated reconciliation-and-critical-minerals agenda.*

2. **[First Nation of Nacho Nyäk Dun neutral on possible Eagle Gold Mine sale, slams Yukon gov't handling](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMi5gFBVV95cUxNT0cwUVMtbk9PS2xHcFQxLVl2VkdENF9uQzhkWVcxXzhFQ3E4clUweFVzckFNY1VGWkc1UmFtXzdJYWRENEFkOXFfUmthTm1zdVJHQmNUMHcwdnh0cGxRenVkVVpHTmlzSTdhZ2tERjZiMXdqUVAzMExJbC1SNHhpQXg1ZUFnTHM1Q2p3d3R1dC04cUVaemlFRTZ5Rmk3NXozNld6N1BIMFR0MzRrQjZRQ3RCVkY2ZVFYdURhNkRxRjVwbXVKT2RETkY0c241Skh3X0dmV1gyUzZnMFdzb2lrQ3RhbmVsUQ?oc=5)** — The First Nation of Nacho Nyäk Dun has declared a neutral position on the potential sale of the Eagle Gold Mine while sharply criticizing the Yukon government's management of the sale process, citing inadequate Indigenous consultation as the primary concern.
   *This continues the Eagle Gold Mine saga tracked across the April 28 and April 29 briefs — the First Nation's explicit condemnation of process (rather than outcome) is significant, as it signals that even a successful sale to the Singapore-based suitor could face legal challenge if free, prior, and informed consent obligations are not meaningfully met, adding governance risk to the already-complex Investment Canada Act national security review questions.*
