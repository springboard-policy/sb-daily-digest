## Skills Policy & Workforce Development

1. **C.D. Howe: Express Entry improved immigrant earnings, but temporary resident surge risks undoing gains** — A new C.D. Howe Commentary by Skuterud and Zhang (*Selection Matters: Lessons from Two Decades of Immigrant Earnings*) finds that immigrants admitted after Express Entry's 2015 launch achieved higher earnings at landing and that recent male cohorts are on track to reach earnings parity with Canadian-born workers within five years — but documents a sharp deterioration in hourly earnings among non-permanent resident (NPR) men between 2020–2024, particularly college-educated workers, and warns that category-based selection and NPR-to-PR pathways risk reversing a decade of gains; the authors recommend returning to exclusive reliance on the Comprehensive Ranking System, enhanced with field of study and prior Canadian earnings criteria. [Read the report](https://cdhowe.org/publication/selection-matters-lessons-from-two-decades-of-immigrant-earnings/)
   *Coming as the Carney government recalibrates immigration levels downward after the 2024 caps, this report gives the new Parliament's economic immigration policy debate a 20-year empirical anchor — reinforcing that selection design, not just volume, determines whether immigration delivers productivity gains.*

2. **FSC research finds EDI backlash narrative overstated — most Canadian workers support inclusive workplace practices** — A Future Skills Centre–funded report (*EDI Backlash? What Canadian Workers Really Think*, Environics Institute and Diversity Institute, April 2026) finds 54% of Canadian workers view workplace EDI positively, only 16% negatively, and that nearly half report their employer's EDI approach has benefited their own opportunities; crucially, support is highest among economically secure workers, suggesting that financial precarity — not EDI itself — drives opposition. [Read the report](https://fsc-ccf.ca/projects/edi-backlash/)
   *The findings directly push back against the U.S.-driven rollback narrative that has influenced some Canadian employers and political rhetoric, and provide empirical grounding for workforce development organizations defending inclusive hiring practices — particularly relevant as racialized workers are projected to make up 42–47% of the working-age population by 2041.*

3. **Ontario's Northlander Rideshare Pilot framed as Uber subsidy at below-minimum-wage rates** — A CCPA analysis argues that Ontario's forthcoming Northlander Rideshare Pilot — set to launch spring/summer 2026 alongside the Northlander train revival — exploits the province's *Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act* "engaged time" loophole to allow Uber to pay drivers as little as $5.97/hour in median net earnings, and is positioned to become a province-wide rideshare regulatory framework by 2027. [Read the analysis](https://www.policyalternatives.ca/news-research/ontario-is-getting-ready-to-hand-uber-a-massive-gift/)
   *This is the sharpest provincial test yet of whether the gig economy labour floor established by the DPWRA is a floor or a ceiling — and sets a precursor for federal gig-work classification debates the Carney government has yet to address in a new mandate.*

4. **C.D. Howe podcast: $10/day childcare promise stalled by supply-side gap** — Economists Parisa Mahboubi and Tingting Zhang (C.D. Howe) highlight that despite federal commitments of 250,000 new childcare spaces, waitlists are growing because demand generated by affordability improvements has outpaced space creation, with supply-side constraints — including workforce shortages in the early childhood sector — as the primary bottleneck. [Listen and read](https://cdhowe.org/publication/why-the-10-day-childcare-program-isnt-working-how-to-fix-it-parisa-mahboubi-tingting-zhang/)
   *The ECE workforce shortage is a persistent skills-pipeline failure that connects directly to the federal childcare transfer agreements — and the Carney majority now has the political runway to revisit funding conditionality and labour market supports for the sector if it chooses to act.*
