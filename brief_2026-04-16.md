## Skills Policy & Workforce Development

1. **FSC Research: Most Canadian Workers Support EDI — "Backlash" Narrative Overstated** — A new Future Skills Centre report, *EDI Backlash? What Canadian Workers Really Think*, drawing on a national survey of 5,603 workers, finds 54% of Canadian employees view workplace EDI positively, only 16% negatively, and nearly half report their employer's EDI approach has *benefited* their own opportunities. ([FSC](https://fsc-ccf.ca/projects/edi-backlash/))
   *The finding that EDI opposition is concentrated among economically insecure workers — not a broad majority — is significant counter-evidence to the political framing that has prompted some Canadian employers to quietly scale back inclusion programs in response to U.S. corporate rollbacks; it strengthens the evidence base for organizations defending EDI programming in government and employer settings.*

2. **C.D. Howe: Express Entry Worked — But Temporary Resident Earnings Deterioration Threatens Gains** — A new C.D. Howe Commentary (*Selection Matters*, Skuterud & Zhang) finds that post-2015 Express Entry reforms improved labour market outcomes for permanent residents — with recent male cohorts approaching earnings parity with Canadian-born workers within five years — but documents a sharp deterioration in earnings among non-permanent resident men (2020–2024), particularly college-educated workers. ([C.D. Howe](https://cdhowe.org/publication/selection-matters-lessons-from-two-decades-of-immigrant-earnings/))
   *With the Carney government's immigration reduction targets expected to turn population growth negative in coming years, this report arrives as a direct brief to Ottawa: return to exclusive reliance on the Comprehensive Ranking System and resist using temporary-resident-to-PR pathways as a volume mechanism, or risk dismantling a decade of hard-won gains in immigrant labour market integration.*

3. **C.D. Howe: $10/Day Childcare Not Delivering Promised Spaces — Structural Fix Needed** — A new C.D. Howe podcast and associated report (*From Promise to Practice*) by Mahboubi and Zhang finds that while fee reductions have improved affordability, Canada's promised 250,000+ new childcare spaces have not materialized, with demand substantially outpacing supply and families stuck on waitlists. ([C.D. Howe](https://cdhowe.org/publication/why-the-10-day-childcare-program-isnt-working-how-to-fix-it-parisa-mahboubi-tingting-zhang/))
   *This adds to growing evidence — including from FINA pre-budget submissions — that the federal childcare program's design prioritizes demand-side subsidies over supply-side capacity building; the Carney majority now faces pressure to address the structural gap before the next federal budget.*

4. **CCPA: Ontario's Northlander Rideshare Pilot a Gig-Work Wage Subsidy for Uber** — CCPA analysis reveals that Ontario's upcoming Northlander Rideshare Pilot — framed as rural transit support alongside the Northlander train revival — will extend the *Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act*'s "engaged time only" wage model provincewide, with U of T research showing Uber drivers' median after-expense earnings at $5.97/hour in 2024. The pilot, led by Cabinet Office staff and linked to Uber's lobbyist Rubicon, is expected to launch summer 2026 as a template for a 2027 province-wide framework. ([CCPA](https://www.policyalternatives.ca/news-research/ontario-is-getting-ready-to-hand-uber-a-massive-gift/))
   *This is the most concrete provincial development in the ongoing platform/gig work policy debate; the provincewide standardization timeline means federal and provincial advocates for gig worker protections have a narrow window ahead of the 2027 framework rollout — and it sets a low-road precedent that other provinces may follow.*

## Social Assistance & Income Security

1. **LNG Canada Flared More Gas in 2025 Than Any Recorded LNG Terminal Globally in 2024** — An investigation by The Narwhal and Point Source finds LNG Canada burned 350 million cubic metres of gas in 2025 — roughly 50% above estimates for the world's highest-flaring LNG terminal in 2024 (Nigeria's Bonny Island) — with repeated exceedances of provincial permits and ongoing mechanical failures at the $40B Kitimat facility. ([The Narwhal](https://thenarwhal.ca/lng-canada-burned-gas/))
   *While primarily an environmental story, the emissions and community health impacts on Kitimat — a northern BC town of ~8,000 — raise questions about the adequacy of Indigenous and community consultation and federal/provincial oversight mechanisms for major resource infrastructure, with implications for how "clean LNG" claims are used to justify future Arctic and northern development approvals.*

2. **Carney Government's Access-to-Information Reforms Draw Sharp Pushback** — The Narwhal reports that Treasury Board's March 5 proposal to redefine "official records" — potentially shielding routine emails and text messages from ATI requests — has prompted the Information Commissioner and public access advocates to call on a parliamentary committee to "urgently" review the changes before they advance; the public feedback period runs to June 15. ([The Narwhal](https://thenarwhal.ca/canada-access-to-information-changes/))
   *Restricting what constitutes an official government record would most directly harm researchers, journalists, and advocacy organizations that rely on ATI to document policy implementation gaps — including in income support, Indigenous services, and disability programming — making this a sleeper issue for accountability-focused civil society organizations.*

3. **C.D. Howe Immigration-Earnings Study Has Direct Implications for Social Support Gaps** — The same C.D. Howe study (see Skills section) documents deteriorating earnings among non-permanent resident men — especially college-educated workers — between 2020 and 2024; this population is largely ineligible for EI, social assistance, and other income supports, creating structural vulnerability that income security advocates have flagged but that federal reform has not yet addressed. ([C.D. Howe](https://cdhowe.org/publication/canadas-immigration-gains-were-driven-by-selection-maintaining-them-is-now-the-challenge/))
   *The sharp earnings deterioration among this group — concurrent with exclusion from the social safety net — represents a growing hole in Canada's income security architecture that the Carney government's immigration reduction targets alone will not close; the FINA pre-budget consultation window (open until April 30) is the immediate venue to raise it.*

4. **CCPA: B.C. Public Funding of Private Schools "Indefensible" as Public School Share of GDP Declines** — CCPA argues that B.C.'s continued public funding of private schools is unjustifiable as public school funding as a share of GDP falls, with equity and accessibility implications for lower-income families dependent on public education. ([CCPA](https://www.policyalternatives.ca/news-research/public-funding-for-b-c-s-private-schools-is-indefensible/))
   *This reflects a national pattern — documented in Ontario last week by CCPA — of cumulative public education disinvestment that creates downstream skill and social mobility gaps; the federal–provincial transfer framework for education is not indexed in ways that constrain this drift.*
